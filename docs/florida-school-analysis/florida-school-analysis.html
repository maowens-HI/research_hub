Florida Private School Access and Demographics: 2000 Analysis

Published

December 12, 2025

Analysis Overview

This document presents cross-sectional analysis of the relationship between Florida community demographics and private school access for the year 2000. The analysis examines PUMA-level demographic characteristics (income, race, ethnicity, education) and their association with private school exposure measures within a 5-mile radius.

Key Features:

  • Geographic Unit: Public Use Microdata Areas (PUMAs)
  • Distance Buffer: 5-mile radius (primary specification)
  • School Levels: Elementary, middle, and high school combined
  • Time Period: Year 2000 Census cross-section
Methodology Note

School exposure measures are constructed at the Census block level using geocoded school coordinates, then aggregated to PUMA geography. Demographic measures come from IPUMS 2000 Census 5% sample.

Variable Definitions (Percent vs. Proportion Explicit)

Percent Units (0–100 scale)

These variables are already expressed in percentage points.

  • hisp_share: Percent Hispanic
  • black_share: Percent Black
  • white_share: Percent White

Proportion Units (0–1 scale)

Multiply by 100 to interpret as percent.

  • less_hs: Share with less than high school
  • hs_grad: Share with high school diploma
  • some_college: Share with some college
  • college_plus: Share with a college degree or higher

Raw Continuous Variables

No scaling.

  • avg_income: Average household income (2000 dollars)
  • avg_age: Mean age
  • total_school: Average private school count per block within the PUMA (5-mile radius)
  • relig / non_relig: Counts of religious vs. non-religious schools
  • distinct_fine: Number of distinct fine-grained school types
  • distinct_collapsed: Number of distinct collapsed school categories

Religious vs. Non-Religious Schools

Non-religious schools show notably different patterns from religious schools:

  • Income: Non-religious schools are significantly associated with higher income areas (coef=728, p<0.05), while religious schools show no income relationship
  • Hispanic share: Non-religious schools show much stronger correlation with Hispanic population (coef=2.45 vs 0.85)
  • Education: Non-religious schools strongly associated with college-educated areas (coef=0.006, p<0.01); religious schools show no relationship

Interpretation Guide

This regression answers: “After controlling for race, ethnicity, age, and education, do areas with more private schools have higher incomes?”

  • total_school coefficient (58.16, n.s.): After controlling for demographics, private school count has no significant relationship with area income
  • hs_grad coefficient (-224,711*):** Areas with higher shares of residents whose highest education is high school (vs. less or more) have dramatically lower incomes
  • hisp_share coefficient (-244*):** Higher Hispanic population share associated with lower average income, controlling for other factors
  • avg_age coefficient (928*):** Older populations associated with higher income areas
  • R-squared (0.624): Demographics explain 62% of income variation across PUMAs

Data Sources and Methods

Click to expand: Technical Details

Demographic Data:

  • Source: IPUMS USA 2000 Census 5% sample
  • Variables: Income (household-level), race, ethnicity, education, age (person-level)
  • Weights: hhwt for household variables, perwt for person variables
  • Geography: Public Use Microdata Areas (PUMAs)

School Data:

  • Source: Geocoded private school locations
  • Distance Calculation: geonear package with 5-mile buffer
  • Geographic Construction: Block-level exposure aggregated to PUMA
  • School Classifications:
    • Religious vs. Non-religious
    • Fine-grained: All original denomination categories
    • Collapsed: 10 broad categories

Sample Restrictions:

  • Florida only (statefip==12)
  • Year 2000 (year==2000)
  • 5% Census sample (sample==200001)
  • Distance filter: 5-mile radius (primary specification)

Crosswalk:

  • MCDC Geocorr 2000: Links 2000 Census blocks to 2000 PUMAs
  • Block-level school counts averaged within each PUMA
